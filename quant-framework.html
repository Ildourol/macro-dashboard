2. Multi-Factor Signal Scoring

A narrative ranking ("Dow strongest, Bitcoin weakest") is a starting point, not a system. A systematic desk decomposes each market into independent factors, scores each -2 to +2, and sums so the final rank is reproducible and auditable, not vibes.

Method: Trend = price vs. 50D/200D stack (Trade Setups). Positioning = Insider Week heatmap percentages, commercial net/change, large-spec net/change, small-trader net/change, TFF category nets, and combined index graph snapshots (COT). Flow = options put/call skew direction (Options Activity). Gamma/Vol = whether dealer positioning suppresses (+) or amplifies (-) moves in that instrument's direction (SPX Gamma). The value of the factor model is that it will diverge from the narrative the moment one factor moves independently, which is the actual trade signal to watch for.

3. Position Sizing & Risk Budgeting

"Buy YM, sell NQ" is not a position until it has a size. A quant desk sizes to account risk, not to conviction conviction sets direction, volatility sets size.
Volatility-Targeting Formula
Contracts = (Account Risk Budget) (ATR Point Value)

Example MYM long: if daily ATR 180 points and point value = $0.50, a 1% risk budget on a $50,000 account ($500) sizes to 500 (180 0.50) 5 contracts, not "however many feels right."
Kelly-Fraction Sanity Check
f* = W [(1W)/R], where W = historical win rate, R = avg win/avg loss.

For a mean-reversion setup inside the gamma long-zone (SPX Gamma) with an estimated W55%, R1.2, f* 0.175 even a genuine edge argues for <20% of risk capital on one idea. Most retail accounts should run at a fraction of full Kelly (quarter-Kelly is standard practice) given estimation error in W and R.
Applied to this week's dataset: The Trade Setups page already sizes every idea in MES/MNQ/MYM/M2K/MGC terms specifically so dollar risk per idea can be capped at 12% of equity that convention exists precisely so a string of correlated losses (e.g., NQ and RTY both being long-vol/short-momentum ideas) doesn't compound into an outsized drawdown from what looked like "two separate small trades."

4. Regime Classification

Every technique below only works in the regime it was designed for. Applying a mean-reversion playbook in a trending/breakout regime (or vice versa) is the single most common source of systematic losses. Classify first, then pick the toolkit.
Current Vol Regime
Compressed
VIX 15.81, below 50D/200D avg. Dealer gamma long (+$33.1B). Favors mean-reversion/range strategies over breakout momentum (see Executive Risk Lab).
Current Trend Regime
Rotational
Broad index (SPY) trending; internal leadership rotating hard (Dow vs. Nasdaq). Favors relative-value/spread trades over single-direction index bets.
Current Rates Regime
Bear-Steepener
Long end selling off faster than front end (Rates & SOFR). Historically a value/cyclical tailwind, growth/duration headwind matches the equity rotation exactly.
Playbook selection given this regime stack: Compressed-vol + rotational-trend + bear-steepening argues for (a) range/mean-reversion tactics on ES specifically between the gamma walls, (b) trend-following only on the confirmed leader (YM), never on the laggard (NQ/BTC) until its regime flips, and (c) cheap convexity purchases now rather than after a vol-regime change all three are already the operating conclusions on Trade Setups and Playbook, arrived at here independently through regime classification rather than narrative.

5. Correlation & Hedge Construction

The highest-conviction idea in this terminal (long YM, financed by ES/SPX downside convexity see Playbook) is a relative-value trade, not two independent bets. A quant desk explicitly checks the hedge ratio rather than assuming a 1:1 offset.

YM vs. NQ~0.75Still positively correlated, just less so the "long YM / reduced NQ" rotation trade is a bet on the spread narrowing its correlation gap short-term, not on NQ going to zero. Both legs can still fall together in a genuine risk-off shock.

Key discipline: Correlations documented here are typical/historical approximations for context, not this-week's measured values (this terminal does not have a rolling-correlation data feed) treat them as a reason to size the "spread trade" conservatively, not as a precise hedge ratio to lever up on.

7. Execution Discipline Checklist

Pre-Trade
Factor score computed, not just narrative
Position sized to volatility, not conviction
Regime confirmed compatible with the strategy type
Correlation to existing book checked
Invalidation level defined before entry (see Trade Setups for every level used this week)
In-Trade
No averaging into a position that has breached its invalidation level
Re-check the regime classification if a cross-asset driver moves (e.g., a 10Y yield break)
Treat COT/gamma updates as new information, not confirmation bias fuel
Post-Trade
Log whether the factor score, not just the P&L, was right
Distinguish a losing trade with a correct process from a lucky win with a broken one
Update the factor weights only after enough samples not after one trade
Why this matters more than the individual trade ideas: Every setup on Trade Setups can be right for the wrong reason or wrong for the right reason. The discipline checklist is what separates a repeatable process from a single good call it's the actual skill being demonstrated in this terminal, not any one ticker's direction.
